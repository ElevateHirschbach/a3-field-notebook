  • A slip-seat relay shuttle — two drivers meet in the middle, swap trucks, and each returns home the same day

Objective

Purpose of this walk is to see reality — not to fix. Validate how Expedited operations actually work versus how they are planned, documented, and described.

✅ In Scope
  • Expedited driver support & dispatch operations
  • End-to-end flow of a load within the relay model
  • Relay / swap execution and driver experience
  • AS400 / EPM screen usage and workarounds
  • Brief, debrief, and shift handoff mechanics
  • Information flow, interruptions, and rework cycles
🚫 Explicitly Out of Scope
  • Solution design
  • Technology requests or builds
  • Staffing decisions
  • Organizational changes
  • Any countermeasure commitments during the walk
Rules for the Walk — Own It
  • No fixing during observation
  • No judging individuals — judge the system
  • Write down what you see, not what you think it means
  • Facts first. Interpretation later.
  • Every observation is a question, not a conclusion
  • Ask "why does this happen?" not "who made this happen?"
What to Track Every Day
  • Information Flow: Patterns, constraints, flow stoppage, rework cycles
  • Load Lifecycle: Tender → assign → execute → complete → release
  • Relay/Swap & Driver Experience: Swap timing, driver comms, tablet usability, workarounds

Physical setup, ergonomics, and leadership presence on the floor
Medium Impact
What Was Observed
  • July 13 was the first required on-site day following a remote-to-onsite transition. Approx. 1 hour per person required to reach full operational status — due to inconsistent workstation setup (monitors, docking stations, peripherals).
  • Team works 12-hour shifts requiring a 3-monitor setup to operate efficiently. Shared desks require reconfiguration each shift — compounding start-of-day delays.

  • ⚠ Q Is there a workspace standardization standard (monitor config, docking setup, chair spec) that governs the onsite environment? Who owns it?

~1 hr × headcount lost to workstation setup on transition day. Ongoing daily time loss from shared desk reconfiguration at each shift start. Ergonomic discomfort on 12-hr shifts creates potential for error and absenteeism.

Return to onsite is a reset opportunity. Standardized workstations + consistent leadership floor presence accelerates collaboration, reinforces expectations, and rebuilds team cohesion. The conditions that make Expedited work — tight coordination, fast decisions, real-time coaching — all improve with physical presence.

T2
EPM — System Performance & Usability
Expedited Planning Module: observed bugs, workflow friction, and integration gaps
High Impact
What Was Observed — Integration & Data Flow
  • One-way integration: EPM sends data to AS400, but AS400 does not return updates to EPM. All corrections must be executed in EPM to avoid data discrepancies — there is no rollback from the AS400 side.
  • Dummy appointment workaround: Loads cannot be planned in EPM unless delivery times exist in AS400. Team uses a "0022" dummy appointment to proceed, then must return later to update actual times — creating structured rework on every affected load.
  • T-call dispatch limitations: T-call dispatch requires both legs to exist in EPM. When a load has not been planned in EPM, AS400 is used instead — resulting in dual-system execution and increased error risk.
  • Auto-set logic dependency: EPM automatically sets Pre-T Calls (PTCs) and drops, but only when prerequisite data (PTCs, stop sequence) is fully configured. Loads planned before prerequisites are established cause misordered stops and downstream corrections.
  • Lane 147 multi-stop pathing: EPM pathing for multi-stop lanes (CA → TX) is non-linear, requiring manual reordering of PTC stops. The module appears optimized for point-to-point moves with a mid-swap — creating usability gaps for complex lanes.
  • Manual PTC entry time: When auto-set logic cannot be used, each Pre-T Call location, date, and time must be entered manually (~2–3 min per stop). Subsequent PTCs are calculated manually at +6 hr increments — repetitive and time-consuming on Lane 147.
  • ⚠ Q Is there a documented plan to enable two-way data sync between EPM and AS400? Who owns that decision?
  • ⚠ Q Who owns cleanup of dummy "0022" appointments — and is that tracked or audited?
What Was Observed — Bugs & UX Friction
  • Sidebar collapse: Every entry into EPM requires manually collapsing the sidebar to view all planning lines. This is a required non-value-add click on every single module entry.
  • AM/PM entry error: A load was incorrectly planned at 17:00 instead of 05:00. The error blocked correct dispatch — resolution required waiting until the driver was removed from the load before the EPM entry could be deleted and replanned.
  • Unplan destroys T-calls: Unplanning a shuffled load in EPM inadvertently deletes associated T-calls and planned legs. Re-planning then triggers an error: "load not available." No warning is presented before the destructive action.
  • Search bug: Search function requires users to delete and slowly re-enter numbers to avoid returning all loads instead of the searched item.
  • Row zeroing bug: Randomly, the bottom row of the scheduling interface zeros out or switches to the wrong day — causing hard-to-diagnose planning errors.
  • Backward-planning bug: Planning backward when "zeros" are present on the second row causes an error; planning forward corrects it.
  • Layout inefficiency: The planning button bar occupies excessive screen space, forcing excessive scrolling. Search bar is not auto-focused on open. Concerns raised about blank space and layout density.
  • Visibility window: Team needs visibility to the prior 36 hours and forward 36 hours to match driver timelines. Current view is insufficient.
  • "Express" function: Mixed guidance on whether to use it — some told not to use it, no documented adoption decision, no SOPs.
  • Truck number field: Auto-populates from previous leg — must be manually deleted when planning a new leg, or errors result.
  • ⚠ Q Is there an active EPM bug log and a product owner responsible for these issues? What is the resolution timeline?
  • ⚠ Q Has a formal go/no-go decision been made on "Express"? Who owns that decision and the accompanying SOPs?
  • ⚠ Q Can the EPM sidebar be defaulted to collapsed, and the search bar auto-focused? These are low-effort, high-frequency time saves.
Ideas That Came Out of Observation
  • Fishbowl concept: Customer service could flag loads as "EXP" (expedited) in EPM, auto-populating a list from which planners drag and drop loads onto trucks — eliminating manual search and reducing missed freight.
  • Auto-detect workday: Plan to auto-detect workdays when drivers check in at start, auto-fill pay without manual entry. Missed check-in = load flagged as missed scheduled shift. Reduces PTO/time-off exception volume.
  • Geofence-triggered plans: Suggestion for system to automatically execute plans based on geofence arrival rather than manual dispatch triggers.
  • Driver visibility to swap partner: Drivers need to see swap partner info and ETAs after start-of-day; partner distance should populate after arrival context.
  • Automatic load notifications: Centralized EPM notification 2 hours prior to start time with load and truck details; auto-update handling on changes.

T4
Planning, Dispatch & The Spreadsheet Problem
Manual data flow, Excel as system of record, and the firefighting posture
High Impact
What Was Observed
  • The team primarily uses a SharePoint Excel spreadsheet for daily operations management. Data is manually pulled from AS400 and entered into the spreadsheet each shift.
  • The spreadsheet is treated as the system of record ("the Bible") — it highlights duplicates, tracks loads, drivers, breaks, and status. EPM is the dispatch tool, but the spreadsheet governs the day.
  • Manual copy time measured per lane:
    Lane 147 (CA/TX): ~4.5 hours/day  |  Lane 155 (OK/TN): ~2.0 hours/day  |  Lane 148 (IN/NC): ~2.5 hours/day
  • Color-coding/symbols in 147 spreadsheet: Blue font = planned in EPM; Bold = needs truck assignment; Peach shading = driver assigned; Gray strikethrough = leg complete. This system is understood by the day shift but not standardized or documented.
  • Planners aim to stay half a day ahead — planning the afternoon in the morning and next morning in the evening. Due to system complexity and frequent changes, planning rarely extends further to avoid wasted rework.
  • Team is consistently in "firefighting mode" — reacting to immediate issues rather than planning 2–4 days ahead. This causes missed freight opportunities when competitors have already claimed available loads.
  • OTR planners often find loads scheduled 2–3 days in advance; expedited needs can change within hours. The team struggles to compete for last-minute freight with a reactive posture.
  • Brian uses self-built macros to automate daily truck checks, driver mile reviews, and templated messaging — significantly reducing manual work. Not standardized across the team.
  • Team searches for available loads via the internal "AOS work with planning" screen — looking for expedited loads not already claimed by OTR teams — rather than external load boards.
  • An automation is reportedly being developed by John to auto-tag and guarantee specific loads for the Expedited team, preventing OTR from claiming them.
  • ⚠ Q Why is the Excel spreadsheet still the system of record if EPM is the designated dispatch tool? What functionality gap is it filling?
  • ⚠ Q Has anyone quantified the total daily manual copy hours across all lanes and sized the automation savings opportunity?
  • ⚠ Q Can Brian's macros be documented and standardized for the full team? What is the process for that?
  • ⚠ Q What are the guardrails for when a planner stops searching and escalates to the spot board? Is there a defined threshold (e.g., X hours before departure)?

HOS, Split Shifts & Disruption Recovery
Policy gaps, GeoTab configuration, and unplanned event response
High Impact
What Was Observed
  • Multiple HOS disruption scenarios observed due to a local festival causing 3+ hour traffic delays, burning 2–4 on-duty hours and forcing dispatch to reassign loads to maintain compliance and meet delivery commitments.
  • Team expressed a desire to use the split-shift option in these disruption scenarios but stated it was "not allowed" for Expedited operations.
  • Post-observation follow-up with Chad Smith: Chad was unaware this belief existed on the floor. The expectation is for drivers to return home for a full 10-hour break after each shift when on schedule — making split shifts unnecessary under normal conditions. Chad did not confirm a formal prohibition.
  • GeoTab rule set: Split breaks are currently disallowed for Expedited in GeoTab. When drivers log "sleeper berth," the system converts it to "off duty," preventing a valid split break and potentially causing violations. The workaround is manual log editing by drivers.
  • Disruption types observed: festival traffic, weather delays, swap-location maintenance delays, tire failures. Each can consume 2–4 on-duty hours.
  • ⚠ Q Is the split-shift prohibition a formal documented policy — or a floor assumption that has never been validated? This requires leadership clarification and a written standard.
  • ⚠ Q If split shifts are ever appropriate as a recovery tool, what are the exact conditions, approval chain, and GeoTab configuration needed to support it correctly?
  • ⚠ Q Who owns the GeoTab rule set for Expedited, and what is the process to request a rule change?
  • ⚠ Q Drivers are currently editing logs manually to correct sleeper berth → off duty conversions. Is this a compliance risk? Has legal/safety reviewed it?

T12
Strategy Alignment & Value Proposition
The gap between what Expedited is supposed to be and what it currently operates as
High Impact
Operational Alignment — A Call to Reset

After this visit, I believe we as a company need to reset on Expedited. Before going deeper into analysis, we first need alignment on two foundational questions — what Expedited is, and why we are doing it. Without shared answers, every downstream decision risks being directionally wrong.

1. What is Expedited?
What differentiates it from OTR, Dedicated, or Regional fleets? Clarifying this narrows our focus, aligns the group, and lets us scope analysis against a defined current state and a defined future state — rather than trying to evaluate everything.
  • ⚠ Q Is Expedited a slip-seat operation running from E-Call to L-Call?
  • ⚠ Q Is it focused on the middle leg, with locals/regionals handling pickup and delivery?
  • ⚠ Q Is it a team operation?
2. Why are we doing Expedited?
What role is Expedited intended to play for the organization? Answering this is equally important — it keeps our analysis focused on what actually matters.
  • ⚠ Q Is the goal to improve OTR profitability?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to maximize utilization?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to provide a premium, high-service fleet for customers?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to support our autonomous strategy?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to become our most profitable business segment?
Core Challenge — Alignment Gap
Right now, I am not confident we have alignment on these questions. The prevailing sentiment appears to be: "This is important because Richard said so, and since traditional OTR wasn't working, this is the direction we're pursuing to improve — however that's defined." Without a shared definition of what Expedited is and why it exists, we cannot effectively diagnose gaps or build a credible future state.
Desired State — A Winnable Strategy
If we can clearly define both the what and the why, we can narrow the scope of our analysis, avoid overextending ourselves, and use our time more effectively. The goal is a winnable strategy — one where we are aligned, able to execute effectively, and positioned to succeed as an organization.
Strategic Gap — Observed
  • Expedited currently operates as a mix of models (A, B, and C) with no single aligned strategy — teams are moving in different directions rather than toward a common objective.
  • Lanes with consistent committed customer volume (e.g., CC 148 IN/NC — Pepsi) execute reliably and predictably. These are the lanes that work.
  • Lanes without sufficient volume require freight to be borrowed from other lanes or divisions. This masks the underlying demand gap, introduces inefficiency, and is structurally incompatible with the autonomous operations pathway.
  • Only ~5% of current freight fits true end-to-end expedited (Model A). Current operations are at Model C, moving toward Model B — with long-term aim of Model A for select freight.
  • Coverage includes three AM and three PM drivers across staggered Mon–Fri, Sat–Tue, Wed–Sun schedules. One driver out ~3 months (medical); one new driver added.
  • Network described by team as an "octopus" — expanding in many directions simultaneously while strategy continues to evolve. This creates a lack of clear, consistent focus with priorities frequently shifting.
  • A driver asked post-orientation: "What's the point of Expedited?" — specifically, what value does the customer actually see? The team could not consistently answer this question.
  • Best observed articulation of the value proposition: Expedited is not just "faster freight" — it is the entry point to autonomous transportation. Customers like Anheuser-Busch, Pepsi, and Walmart suppliers have sustainability and innovation commitments; partnering in the "crawl" phase gives them early access to technology that will define freight's future. This framing creates strategic differentiation and gets leadership attention.
  • We want Expedited to be the "Hirschbach Elite Team" of drivers — but current hiring criteria do not align with that positioning.
  • ⚠ Q Has a formal decision been made on which lanes should be Model A target lanes vs. Model B/C support lanes — and is that communicated to the operations team?
  • ⚠ Q Does a written Expedited value proposition exist that can be used consistently in customer conversations and driver orientation?
  • ⚠ Q What hiring profile defines an "Elite Fleet" driver — and does the current screening process select for it?
  • ⚠ Q If the strategy is to position Expedited as the crawl phase of autonomous, which customers should we be targeting right now — and is Sales aligned on that list?
