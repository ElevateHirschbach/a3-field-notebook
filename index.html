Background Brief
Current state — as of early March 2026
What Expedited is supposed to be
  • A slip-seat relay shuttle — two drivers meet in the middle, swap trucks, and each returns home the same day
  • Every driver starts and ends at their home yard. No one lives in the truck.
  • The truck never stops moving: AM and PM departure waves, two full round trips daily per truck
  • Designed to be 100% self-contained — no OTR support, no local truck feeding the shuttle, no borrowed freight
  • Financial thesis: drivers home nightly → lower turnover. Truck runs 2 shifts → 2× asset utilization vs. standard OTR
  • Long-term: relay model runs consistent routes, known lanes, fixed swap points — the exact profile needed for autonomous trucks when Aurora technology comes online
Four Distinct Operations Inside Expedited

Expedited is not a single operation. It is four distinct operations grouped under one division, each with its own freight profile, driver model, and planning discipline. When discussing Expedited performance, staffing, strategy, or financials, these areas need to be addressed separately — conflating them masks what is actually working and what is not.

Core Tension
What we were sold vs. what we actually built
What it was supposed to be
  • A slip-seat relay shuttle — two drivers meet at a midpoint, swap trucks, each returns home the same day
  • 100% self-contained — no OTR support, no local truck feeding the shuttle, no borrowed freight
  • Drop-and-hook 24/7 with 12-hour appointment windows — take-or-pay contracts, guaranteed freight
  • ~450-mile round trip — what a driver can comfortably handle inside an 11-hour clock
  • ~$2.20 per total mile inclusive of fuel surcharge — consistent daily volume, 7 days a week, 52 weeks a year
  • Truck runs two shifts → 2× asset utilization vs. standard OTR; drivers home nightly → lower turnover
  • ~40% Aurora-compatible — the entry point to autonomous trucking when the technology comes online
  • Blue Angel driver standard — top-tier professionalism, urgency, precision, total commitment to excellence
What it actually is
  • 94% of orders on key cost centers depend on non-EXP trucks for pickup or delivery — most need both. Only ~6% fit the original self-contained model
  • A mix of Models A, B, and C with no single aligned strategy — team described the network as an "octopus" expanding in many directions at once
  • Not all freight is drop-and-hook — live loads are adding delay; lanes without volume require freight pulled from other lanes/divisions to build the load
  • Out-of-route miles routinely planned against the 300-mile threshold rather than treated as exception — one observed pattern: 200 OOR × 232 occurrences = ~48,000 extra miles
  • Manual spreadsheet is the system of record — 4.5 hrs/day on Lane 147 alone; ~11 months on EPM and basic day-to-day still cannot be done in the tool
  • Team in firefighting mode — 5–6 hrs/day per person on manual processes, 2-day onboarding, no SOPs, single dispatcher at the center of every decision
  • Network design and capacity are not aligned with Expedited points — Memphis ~220-mile average deadhead, Cocoa FL ~224, Liberal KS / Amarillo no truck landing pattern at all
  • Service declined in late 2025 and has not recovered as Expedited fleet grew from 46 → 60+ trucks; T-calls and dispatches per truck climbing in lockstep
  • $61,402 in extra pay over 15 weeks (~$4K/week) — XP, MX, BD, DP, LA, incentive — much of it covering structural gaps the original model was supposed to eliminate
  • "Elite Fleet" hiring criteria do not match the orientation language — no formal selection process beyond baseline requirements

Strategy Alignment & Value Proposition
The gap between what Expedited is supposed to be and what it currently operates as
High Impact
Operational Alignment — A Call to Reset

After this visit, I believe we as a company need to reset on Expedited. Before going deeper into analysis, we first need alignment on two foundational questions — what Expedited is, and why we are doing it. Without shared answers, every downstream decision risks being directionally wrong.

1. What is Expedited?
What differentiates it from OTR, Dedicated, or Regional fleets? Clarifying this narrows our focus, aligns the group, and lets us scope analysis against a defined current state and a defined future state — rather than trying to evaluate everything.
  • ⚠ Q Is Expedited a slip-seat operation running from E-Call to L-Call?
  • ⚠ Q Is it focused on the middle leg, with locals/regionals handling pickup and delivery?
  • ⚠ Q Is it a team operation?
2. Why are we doing Expedited?
What role is Expedited intended to play for the organization? Answering this is equally important — it keeps our analysis focused on what actually matters.
  • ⚠ Q Is the goal to improve OTR profitability?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to maximize utilization?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to provide a premium, high-service fleet for customers?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to support our autonomous strategy?
  • ⚠ Q Is it to become our most profitable business segment?
Core Challenge — Alignment Gap
Today, Expedited operates as a mix of different approaches without a single aligned strategy. As a result, the teams are moving in different directions instead of marching toward a common objective. Without a shared definition of what Expedited is and why it exists, we cannot effectively diagnose gaps or build a credible future state.
Desired State — A Winnable Strategy
If we can clearly define both the what and the why, we can narrow the scope of our analysis, avoid overextending ourselves, and use our time more effectively. The goal is a winnable strategy — one where we are aligned, able to execute effectively, and positioned to succeed as an organization.
Strategic Gap — Observed
  • Expedited currently operates as a mix of models (A, B, and C) with no single aligned strategy — teams are moving in different directions rather than toward a common objective.
  • Lanes with consistent committed customer volume (e.g., CC 148 IN/NC — Pepsi) execute reliably and predictably. These are the lanes that work.
  • Lanes without sufficient volume require freight to be borrowed from other lanes or divisions. This masks the underlying demand gap, introduces inefficiency, and is structurally incompatible with the autonomous operations pathway.
  • Only ~5% of current freight fits true end-to-end expedited (Model A). Current operations are at Model C, moving toward Model B — with long-term aim of Model A for select freight.
  • Coverage includes three AM and three PM drivers across staggered Mon–Fri, Sat–Tue, Wed–Sun schedules. One driver out ~3 months (medical); one new driver added.
  • Network described by team as an "octopus" — expanding in many directions simultaneously while strategy continues to evolve. This creates a lack of clear, consistent focus with priorities frequently shifting.
  • A driver asked post-orientation: "What's the point of Expedited?" — specifically, what value does the customer actually see? The team could not consistently answer this question.
  • Best observed articulation of the value proposition: Expedited is not just "faster freight" — it is the entry point to autonomous transportation. Customers like Anheuser-Busch, Pepsi, and Walmart suppliers have sustainability and innovation commitments; partnering in the "crawl" phase gives them early access to technology that will define freight's future. This framing creates strategic differentiation and gets leadership attention.
  • We want Expedited to be the "Hirschbach Elite Team" of drivers — but current hiring criteria do not align with that positioning.
  • ⚠ Q Has a formal decision been made on which lanes should be Model A target lanes vs. Model B/C support lanes — and is that communicated to the operations team?
  • ⚠ Q Does a written Expedited value proposition exist that can be used consistently in customer conversations and driver orientation?
  • ⚠ Q What hiring profile defines an "Elite Fleet" driver — and does the current screening process select for it?
  • ⚠ Q If the strategy is to position Expedited as the crawl phase of autonomous, which customers should we be targeting right now — and is Sales aligned on that list?
Value Proposition — Three Lenses Tested Against Reality

Three potential value propositions for Expedited were evaluated against what was observed on the floor. Each was tested for whether it actually holds up under current operating conditions.

Value LensWhat Was ObservedHolds Up?
1. Speed
"We move freight faster"		 Speed is situational. It clearly matters when time is critical (e.g., Texas → California in 36 hours instead of 3–4 days). However, not all customers place a premium on transit speed — for many, speed is not the primary purchasing driver. Situational only
2. Driver home time
"Drivers are home daily"		 Critically important to drivers (a key reason they accept Expedited roles), but home time is not a customer purchasing decision. Customers don't pay a premium because our driver is home at night. Driver value only
3. Financial / OR
"Expedited is more profitable"		 Expedited's OR appears more favorable in part because costs are absorbed by OTR (pickup/delivery, T-call assist, borrowed freight). If all costs were properly allocated, the financial case would likely be less compelling than it currently appears. Distorted by allocation
Reframe — Future-Focused Strategy
Reframed, the value of Expedited is not "moving faster freight" — it is moving freight differently with greater consistency, predictability, and future-focused capabilities. The natural customer fit is companies investing heavily in sustainable transportation technologies — Anheuser-Busch, Pepsi, Walmart suppliers, and similar. The relationship structure should look more like a dedicated contract with a sticky commitment partner than a traditional spot/expedited engagement, anchored on the crawl → walk → run path to autonomy.
Sales Approach — A Customer Conversation That Works
  • The selling motion under this reframe is fundamentally different from current bidding. Example pitch shape:
  • "Walmart, I know you've already bid out all of your lanes for this year. But if you can give me committed volume from Texas to Kansas City on this lane, I can start running it autonomous by the end of the year. I need you to commit to X loads per month in exchange."
  • This pitch works because it offers something the customer cannot get from a typical OTR bid — early access to autonomous capability — in exchange for the only thing Expedited needs to be successful: committed, predictable volume.
  • It also creates a credible reason for a customer to revisit a closed bid mid-cycle, which is normally extremely difficult in transportation sales.
  • ⚠ Q Is Sales currently equipped (with autonomous timeline, lane commitments, contract structure) to have this conversation with target customers? If not, what's the gap?
  • ⚠ Q Has the bid data been reviewed to confirm that what we're bidding on actually aligns with where Expedited can run efficiently today?

Extra Pay — Clarifications & Attribution Call-Outs

Several of the line items above carry attribution caveats that need to be reflected before any conclusions are drawn. These were surfaced during the review and need to be clarified so the financial picture isn't read as Expedited overhead when it isn't.

  • Detention, Layover, and most Breakdown pay on CC 153 is Driscoll's-related, not Expedited operations. Driscoll's is structurally closest to the vest of any Expedited cost center, and the contract carries detention and layover terms that are not present on the other lanes. These should be tagged separately when evaluating Expedited's true operational extra-pay run rate.
  • Breakdown pay on CC 153 includes OTR drivers running in Expedited trucks. When an OTR driver's truck was in the shop and they were temporarily assigned an Expedited unit, any layover or breakdown pay generated during that period landed in the Expedited numbers. The cost is real, but it is not an Expedited operational decision.
  • Driscoll's incentive pay (~$7K over 15 weeks) was not part of the original lane setup. Per the team, the incentive was created when teams were placed on Driscoll's — the lane was not designed to require an additional incentive on top of base pay. This should be reviewed against the current lane economics.
  • Incentive (XP) pay is functioning as a coverage subsidy. Example: Bonnie Brooks earned ~$2,123 over the 15-week period covering Alan's vacation week — Alan was paid PTO and Bonnie was paid for working 14 consecutive days. Several similar patterns appear in the file (Brandon, Reina, others).
  • The 7-on / 7-off schedule itself is the primary hiring incentive for Expedited. It is also the structural reason XP is showing up at this volume — when someone takes time off, the only way to cover the shift today is to pay another team member to work an extra rotation. This is a policy issue, not a schedule issue.
  • ⚠ Q Can Driscoll's-related extra pay (DP, LA, BD-OTR-driver) be tagged separately in the source file so Expedited's true operational run-rate is visible?
  • ⚠ Q If the 7x7 schedule stays in place as the hiring lever, what is the right PTO and shift-trade policy to support it without driving XP costs higher? A non-traditional schedule may need a non-traditional PTO policy.
  • ⚠ Q Should the Driscoll's incentive pay be re-baselined against the current lane economics, or grandfathered as part of the original commitment?
